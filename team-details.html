Professional Overview

Two decades of quiet rigour

Maren co-founded Luzen in 2003 with a single conviction: that architecture could be rigorous and warm at once — exacting in its detail, generous in how it holds a life.

Trained at the Oslo School of Architecture and later under Peter Zumthor's studio in Haldenstein, she returned home with an obsession for material truth — the idea that a wall should be what it appears to be, and that light is a building material as real as stone. As Principal Architect she leads the studio's residential and cultural commissions from the first site walk to the final handover, and is known among clients for an unhurried generosity: a willingness to sit with a problem until the obvious answer gives way to the right one.

Her leadership is collaborative rather than authorial. She builds small, deliberate teams around each project and protects the long, quiet hours of testing — sketching at 1:50, walking the site at dusk, returning to a detail until it disappears. Sustainability, for Maren, is not a department but a discipline: buildings sited to need less, materials chosen to age rather than expire, and a structural honesty that lets a house be repaired, not replaced.

Across projects in Norway, Austria, Portugal and Japan, that patience has become the studio's signature. Her Meridian Residence, completed above the Oslofjord in 2025, won the European Architecture Award and has come to define the Luzen voice — quiet, durable, and deeply attuned to its place.

"
Architecture is not about buildings. It is about creating places people remember.

Maren Solvang · Principal Architect

Maren's practice is guided by what she calls "material honesty" — the conviction that every surface, joint, and threshold should express its structural truth. Ornamentation, if it appears at all, is earned rather than applied.

Her buildings are conceived first as atmospheres: the quality of morning light across a concrete wall, the sound of rain on an oak floor, the thermal weight of stone in winter. Programme follows from these sensory commitments, not the other way around.

This is why she returns to the same question at every stage of a project: does this serve the person who will live here? The answer is never structural. It is always human.
