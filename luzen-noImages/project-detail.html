The Meridian Residence sits on a forested hillside above the Oslofjord, a site that demanded both sensitivity and confidence. Our brief was to create a family home that felt simultaneously open to the landscape and deeply sheltered — a contradiction that became the organising principle of the design.

The building is conceived as a series of interconnected volumes that step down the slope, each one calibrated to frame a specific view and admit light at a particular angle. The primary living space opens to a full-height south-facing glazed wall that captures the arc of the winter sun, flooding the interior with warmth during the months when daylight is most precious in Oslo.

South façade at dusk — the full-height glazing captures the arc of the winter sun

Materials were selected for their ability to age gracefully in the Nordic climate: dark-stained pine cladding, raw concrete, and locally quarried limestone. Interiors continue this material language with a palette of smoked oak, linen, and hand-plastered walls — surfaces that invite touch and improve with time.

The landscape design is integral to the architecture, not an afterthought. Existing birch and pine trees are preserved where possible, and new planting uses species native to the region to create a garden that belongs to its place and requires minimal maintenance.

Living room — smoked oak, linen, hand-plastered walls
Kitchen — locally quarried limestone, raw concrete ceiling
Garden terrace — native planting, stepping down toward the fjord
Master bedroom — linen textiles, birch tree views
Staircase — raw concrete, steel cable balustrade
